## the thing about watching films alone at 2am

so there i was. couldn't sleep. scrolling through criterion channel like some kind of film bro cliché, and **Stealing Beauty** (1996) just... appeared. bernardo bertolucci. liv tyler when she still had that *ethereal confused teenager* energy. tuscany looking obscenely pretty.

i almost scrolled past.

thank god i didn't.

## what even is this movie?

here's what happens (no spoilers that matter):
- 19-year-old Lucy arrives in Tuscany
- she's got her dead mother's diary
- there's a mystery about her *real* father
- a house full of artists, poets, dying playwrights
- everyone's slightly pretentious
- everyone's slightly broken
- the light hits everything **wrong** in the right way

but that's not what the movie's *about*.

### it's actually about:

the weird space between being a girl and becoming yourself  
how vulnerability looks different when you choose it  
that specific summer where everything feels significant  
~~virginity as metaphor (yes, really)~~  
the way older people project their lost youth onto the young

## my completely subjective breakdown

| element | my take |

| cinematography | makes me want to cry into a glass of wine i can't afford |

| the male gaze | problematic? yes. also kind of the point? complicated |

## scenes that live in my head rent-free

**the letter writing scene**  
she's composing letters to this boy. crossing out words. trying to sound *deep*. i've never felt so seen. we've all been that person, right? performing depth while actually being deep without knowing it.

```
dear you,
i don't know how to say this
[scratched out]
what i mean is
[scratched out]
fuck, why is honesty so hard
[probably scratched out too]
```

**that conversation under the stars**  
where the dying playwright (jeremy irons being jeremy irons) tells her she's beautiful but doesn't realize it yet. sounds creepy written out. somehow isn't in context. it's more like... witnessing someone recognize something true in the messiest possible way.

**the final scene**  
won't spoil it. but it's about *choosing*. not the choice you expect.

## the stuff that made me uncomfortable (in a good way?)

bertolucci films always have this *thing*—they're beautiful and slightly off. male directors filming young women discovering sexuality = always complicated. but here's what struck me:

Lucy's awkwardness isn't cute-ified. her confusion isn't romanticized into quirky dream girl shit. she makes **stupid decisions**. she misreads situations. she wants things she can't articulate.

that felt... honest?

> "You're so young. You don't know what you want yet."  
> "Maybe I don't. But I know what I *feel*."

(i'm paraphrasing but you get it)

## the poetry scattered throughout

the film's full of poems. some good. some *trying too hard*. some that hit different at 3am:

```
If I could capture beauty
Would I frame it?
Or let it escape
Through my inadequate fingers?
```

*(that's my version of what i remember, probably butchered it)*

## why this film does something to me

it gets the **messiness** of becoming.

not the instagram highlight reel of self-discovery. the actual clumsy stumbling through it. wanting to be deep while being shallow. wanting to be experienced while being terrified. performing adulthood while still feeling like a kid.

Lucy spends the whole film looking for answers:
- who's my father?
- who wrote me that poem?
- why doesn't he notice me?
- when do i become *real*?

but the answer isn't an answer. it's just... continuation. you keep going. you stay awkward. maybe that's the point.

## things i wished i'd known before watching

- [ ] it's slow. *slow* slow. european cinema slow
- [ ] subtitles for some parts (italian/english mix)
- [ ] not a feel-good movie but a *feel everything* movie
- [ ] you might hate the ending. i kind of did. then loved it at 4am
- [ ] bring wine or tea or something to hold onto

## the final thing

**Stealing Beauty** isn't perfect. it's male gaze-y in ways i can't fully defend. the age gaps are *questionable*. some dialogue tries too hard to be profound.

but.

it captures something about being young and lost that most films miss. that specific feeling of thinking you're looking for one thing while actually discovering something else entirely.

also: it made me want to write terrible poetry in tuscany while wearing linen and making bad decisions.

isn't that what good films do? make you want to **live** differently, even just for an afternoon?
